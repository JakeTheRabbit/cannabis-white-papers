Reading the Root ZoneReading the Root Zone

Two questions decide most of a grow: am I over- or under-watering, and how much water does the light actually want? Both come down to reading the root zone. This guide starts with the questions you have to answer before any sensor number means anything — then reads water status three ways, by weight, by probe, and by the plant, with a guidance table and a checklist for every substrate you are likely to run.

Start here

A number is useless without its context

You cannot tell whether you are under-watering from a single number. Water status is three numbers and a plant, read against the substrate the roots sit in.

The two questions — am I over- or under-watering, and how much water does the light want — are the same skill. Both are answered by reading the root zone: how much water is in it, how strong the solution is, how fast it is drying, and whether the plant agrees. Get that right and the sensors stop being a dashboard you stare at and start being instruments you steer with.

The one idea to carry through the whole guide

A number means nothing without the context that makes it readable. 40% moisture in coco is not 40% in rockwool. A probe in the wrong part of a slab reads a different plant than the one you are growing. Every technique below is really about putting that missing context back.

VWC (volumetric water content)the share of the substrate's volume that is water, as a percent. It is the headline number a moisture probe reports — worth only as much as its calibration and its placement.
Drybackhow far the substrate dries between the top of one irrigation and the next, measured as the drop in VWC. Some dryback every cycle is what pulls fresh air — oxygen — back into the root zone. It is also your most useful single signal, and the spine of everything here.
Ask first

Answer these before you diagnose anything

Most watering mistakes are not sensor faults. They are a reading taken without the facts that make it mean something. Nail these down once and every number after gets easier to trust.

A moisture probe reading 42% is not an answer — it is a question. 42% of what substrate, measured by which sensor, sitting where, fed at what strength, under how much light? Change any one of those and 42% flips from healthy to a plant in trouble. Before you touch a setpoint or call a plant over- or under-watered, write down the answers below. This is your baseline card. Keep it near the room.

The one that gets people every time

The last row — the unlogged manual watering. A hose flush or a tank auto-top-up that never made it into the record is the reason a healthy room reads like a sick one, or a sick one reads fine. If a human moved water and it is not written down, the data is telling you a story about a plant that does not exist. Log every off-schedule intervention, or throw the day's read out.

The differential

Over- and under-watering look identical

A drooping plant is not a diagnosis. Three different failures produce the same wilt — and the beginner's instinct, add water, fixes one of them and kills the other two.

Wilt is loss of turgor: the cells lose their internal water pressure and the leaf goes limp. There are three roads to that same limp leaf, and telling them apart is the whole job.

under
Too little water

Real drought. The substrate has given up its available water and the roots have nothing left to pull. VWC is low, the pot is light.

over
Too little air

Drowning. The substrate never dries, so there is no oxygen in the pore space and the roots suffocate and rot. VWC is high and stuck high. It looks like thirst, so the beginner waters again.

EC
Too much salt

Osmotic drought. Plenty of water, but so concentrated the roots cannot pull it in against the salt. EC is high. The plant is thirsty in a wet pot.

Turgorthe water pressure inside plant cells that keeps leaves and stems rigid. Full turgor at lights-on is a plant standing up with its leaves reaching; lost turgor is the droop you are trying to diagnose.

The tie-breaker is the trend, not the snapshot

A VWC of 45% tells you almost nothing on its own. 45% and falling with EC climbing is a plant drying into a salt problem. 45% and stuck there for six hours with EC flat is a plant drowning. Same number, opposite fix. Always read the shape of the curve, not the point.

Know your medium

Your substrate — one page each

The same shot, the same probe number, means something different in each of these media, because they store, release, and re-wet water by different physics. This is the heart of the over/under question. Find the one you run and work from its card.

Two behaviours separate every substrate: how it holds EC (does it buffer the feed, or hand the roots exactly what you poured in), and how it re-wets after it dries (does the next shot soak in evenly, or channel down the wall and out the bottom). Get those two right for your medium and most watering faults never start.

CEC (cation exchange capacity)the substrate's ability to hold nutrient ions on its surfaces and hand them to roots. High in coco and living soil (buffered, forgiving, cal-mag-hungry); effectively zero in rockwool and clay pebbles (inert, precise, unforgiving).
Field capacitythe wettest the substrate holds after free water has drained under gravity — the top of your dryback.
Channelingwhen water carves a shortcut — down a pot wall, through a dry block — and exits as runoff without wetting the bulk of the root zone. It produces the most dangerous false signal in growing: proof of watering over a starving root-ball.

Rockwool / stone wool

Inert, near-zero CEC. It holds no nutrient and buffers nothing — the EC in the slab is the EC you fed, full stop. Huge water-holding capacity at saturation but a steep release curve, and gravity parks a saturated layer at the base under a top that dries first. That vertical gradient is why probe placement in rockwool is make-or-break for your data. Its failure modes are fast and loud: dry it too far and EC spikes hard (no buffer to soak it up), and dry stone wool re-wets badly — water channels through instead of soaking in, so a panicked re-saturation floods the base and drowns the roots you just dried out.

Rockwool checklist
  • Probe in the root mass (upper/middle), never the wet base — the base lies.
  • Know the slab's saturated weight and its dried-back weight; lift to confirm.
  • Watch pore-water EC as it dries — when it hits your ceiling, that VWC is your floor.
  • Ramp P1 with several shots to field capacity; never one big flood.
  • If it dried too far, re-wet with repeated small shots — a single flood re-channels.
  • Run more than one probe per zone; treat a lone outlier as a placement fault.

Coco coir

Behaves like a forgiving soil. High CEC, so it holds and trades nutrients, buffers pH swings, and covers small mistakes — but it binds calcium and magnesium, which is why coco grows carry a permanent cal-mag demand.[5] It wets by capillarity through the whole profile, so a healthy pot is fairly even top to bottom. Its failure modes are quieter and slower: under-watered, salts stack because too little solution moves through; and if it ever goes bone-dry it turns hydrophobic and channels — the next shot runs down the gap between root-ball and pot wall and out the bottom, so you get runoff, tick the box, and the core stays dry.

Coco checklist
  • Probe the middle third, root depth, about one dripper-width off the emitter.
  • Never let it go bone-dry — hydrophobic coco channels and hides a dry core.
  • Track runoff EC vs feed EC as your leaching gauge; creeping up = under-watering.
  • Buffer fresh coco before use; keep cal-mag in every feed.
  • You can run bigger, less frequent shots than rockwool — the buffer forgives.
  • Confirm the core is wet by weight after a shot, not by seeing runoff.

Peat & potting mix (amended / living soil)

A reservoir with a big buffer. Peat and bagged mixes hold a lot of water and, when amended or run as living soil, a lot of nutrient — so EC readings are muddy and less useful, and the medium forgives timing far more than an inert slab does. The catch is the opposite: it is easy to overwater because it dries slowly, and dry peat is the worst re-wetter of the lot — it repels water and needs a slow soak, sometimes a wetting agent, to take it back up. In big pots the buffer is large, so hard crop-steering does not apply; you water, then let it dry a good way down, and let pot weight lead.

Peat / soil checklist
  • Lift test is your primary read — probes are noisy in amended, chunky soil.
  • Water to modest runoff, then let it dry well down; do not keep it wet.
  • If it dried out, re-wet slowly — a fast flood just runs off hydrophobic peat.
  • Living soil: do not over-fertigate or chase EC; the biology handles it.
  • Avoid compaction — it kills the air the roots need.
  • Bigger pot = more buffer = slower cycle; size the watering to the pot, not a table.

Clay pebbles / hydroton (expanded clay, semi-hydro)

The fast, low-margin one. Expanded clay drains almost fully and holds water only as a film plus whatever sits in the reservoir, so there is effectively no dryback buffer — it needs frequent irrigation or continuous recirculation. Inert and zero-CEC, so what the roots see is your reservoir EC, and in a recirculating system that EC drifts as the plant drinks. The upside: it re-wets instantly, never goes hydrophobic, and never channels — the easy substrate to wet, the unforgiving one to leave dry. Its real risk lives in the reservoir: a stalled pump dries roots in minutes, and roots sitting in stagnant warm water rot.

Clay pebbles — guidance bands. High-frequency / recirculating context.
PropertyWhat to expect
Water retentionvery low; drains near-fully; little to no dryback buffer

Clay pebble checklist
  • Pump watchdog and a fallback are mandatory — this substrate fails dry, fast.
  • Monitor reservoir EC, pH, and temperature — not substrate VWC.
  • Keep reservoir and root-zone temp down to hold dissolved oxygen up.
  • Run frequent short cycles; there is no buffer to coast on.
  • Keep drainage clear — stagnant water at the roots means rot.
  • Top up the reservoir to a known EC; do not let it concentrate as it drops.
The two traps that never show up in the headline VWC

Coco and peat lie by omission — they channel or repel, so "I watered to runoff" is not proof the root-ball is wet. Rockwool lies by gradient — the wet base hides a dry, salt-stacked top. Neither trap shows in the VWC number. Both show in the pot weight and the plant.

*VWC and dryback figures on these cards are starting bands, not setpoints. They depend on your sensor's substrate calibration, your genetics, and your steering intent. Dial them by watching the dryback and EC response in your room — never by matching a number off a table.

Read it — the probes

What the probes actually tell you

A substrate probe gives you three numbers — water content, EC, and temperature — and one shape, the dryback curve. The shape is worth more than any of the numbers.

VWC is the headline, and the first thing to know is that it is probably miscalibrated. Capacitance probes like the TEROS 12 ship with a mineral-soil calibration; in coco or stone wool that reads systematically off unless you load the soilless-specific calibration. Grodan's GroSens is calibrated for stone wool out of the box; a TEROS is not. So trust your probe for trends, not for absolute truth, until you have calibrated it to your actual substrate. A probe reading 8 points low still shows you the dryback perfectly.

EC is the number that catches osmotic drought — the wet-pot thirst from Table 2 — before the plant does. Probes report bulk EC, but what the roots feel is the pore-water EC: the strength of the solution in the film around the roots, derived from the bulk reading.[9] The key behaviour: as the substrate dries, pore-water EC rises, because the salts stay behind while the water leaves. Watching pore-water EC climb as you dry back is how you find your safe dryback floor — the moment EC pushes past your ceiling, that VWC is the floor, whatever number it lands on.

Substrate temperature is the quiet third number. A cold slab at lights-on takes up water slowly; a hot root zone drives evaporative demand up and oxygen down. It also sets the vapour-pressure deficit right at the leaf, which — as the light section shows — is half of what decides water use.

EC (electrical conductivity)a proxy for how strong the nutrient solution is — how much salt is dissolved in the water. Rising EC in the substrate means the solution is getting stronger and the roots have to work harder to drink.
Pore-water ECthe EC of the solution actually held in the pores — what the roots taste — as opposed to the raw bulk EC the probe measures. It climbs as the substrate dries even if you added no salt at all.
Placement is the measurement

Put the probe in the middle third of the root zone, at root depth, about one dripper-width off the emitter — never in the wet base, never under the dripper stream, never in the dry edge. Then distrust it anyway: run more than one, and cross-check every reading against pot weight and the plant. A probe records the moisture at one point; you are growing the whole pot.

Log fast enough to see the curve

Sample every one to three minutes, not hourly. The dryback slope, the jump when a shot lands, the overnight decline — the diagnostic value is all in the shape, and an hourly average flattens it into a useless dot.

Read it — the hands

The lift test and the reference plant

The probe drifts and it lies about placement. Weight does neither. This is why the oldest trick in the book still beats the newest sensor for one specific job: total water.

A pot's weight is its water content — directly, with no calibration, no gradient, no channeling blind spot. Know a pot's saturated weight and its dried-back weight and you can feel exactly where in the dryback it sits by lifting it. That is the lift test, and it is not a beginner's crutch you grow out of — it is the calibration reference the pros keep coming back to.

  1. 1
    Learn the two anchor weights
    Weigh a representative pot right after it drains to field capacity, and again at your intended dryback floor. Now every lift in between is a reading.
  2. 2
    Put one plant on a scale
    A representative plant on a bench scale or load cell, logged continuously, is a home-made lysimeter: a calibration-free, gradient-proof dryback curve. For total water it out-truths a VWC probe, because it weighs the whole root zone at once instead of sampling one point.
  3. 3
    Squeeze-test the medium
    Coco: squeeze a handful — free drips means wet, damp-and-cohesive means near field capacity, crumbly-and-light means dry. Rockwool: press the block corner or tilt the slab — free water at saturation, springy-damp is the working band, firm-and-light means you dried it too far.
  4. 4
    Read the runoff
    Volume tells you throughput (aim to see runoff appear, not flood). Then the tell that matters: runoff EC vs feed EC. Runoff much higher than feed = salts stacking = under-watering / under-leaching. Runoff near feed = balanced. Watch it drift day to day.
  5. 5
    Do the channeling check
    Right after a shot, is the pot heavy and the surface evenly damp — or did water hit the tray in seconds while the pot stayed light? Fast runoff plus a light pot means the water channelled and the root-ball is still dry, whatever the probe says.
Lysimeterany setup that weighs a plant-plus-substrate continuously to measure water loss. A single plant on a logging load cell is a perfectly good one, and arguably the highest-value calibration-free instrument in the room.
Build the load-cell reference plant

If you do one thing beyond your probes, put a representative plant on a load cell and log it. It gives you the true dryback with zero calibration drift and no placement error, and it turns "is my VWC probe lying?" into a five-second glance. When the scale and the probe disagree about water, the scale is right — go find out why the probe isn't.

Read it — the plant

Reading the plant

The plant sums up every variable you cannot measure. Learn to read it and it audits your sensors for free, every single day.

Turgor timing is the daily free audit. A well-watered plant is turgid and reaching up at lights-on. A plant that is limp first thing, or that wilts in the back half of the day and only recovers overnight, is telling you the water on offer did not cover the day's demand — either under-watering or a P3 dryback set too deep (both defined in the next section).

Leaf shape splits the two big failures. Upward cupping — the "taco" — with crispy margins and dry tips leans under-watering, heat, or light stress. A soft, puffy, downward droop with no crisping leans over-watering. They look different once you have seen both a few times.

Canopy temperature is a cheap, powerful instrument. A water-stressed plant closes its stomata to stop losing water; with the stomata shut, evaporative cooling stops and the leaf heats up. Point an infrared thermometer at the canopy and compare it to a well-watered reference: a canopy running 2–3 °C hotter than it should is telling you the stomata are closing — the formal version is the crop water stress index.[3][4] The catch is that VPD and airflow move leaf temperature too, so read it against a reference plant, not as an absolute.

CWSI (crop water stress index)a water-stress measure built from how much hotter the canopy runs than a well-watered crop would in the same air. Rising canopy-minus-air temperature means stomata are closing — the plant is rationing water.
The plant confirms; the sensors quantify

When the plant and the probes agree, you are reading truth. When they disagree — plant wilting, probe says wet — the probe is almost always wrong about placement or blind to EC. Believe the plant, then go find which number lied.

The engine

How light sets the water bill

Light is the throttle on the whole water system. Nothing else you control moves water demand as hard — which is exactly why raising it without raising irrigation is the most common way growers under-water a healthy room.

PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux density)the instant intensity of usable (400–700 nm) light landing on the canopy, in µmol/m²/s. How hard the light is hitting, right now.
DLI (daily light integral)the total usable light delivered over the whole photoperiod, in mol/m²/day. DLI = PPFD × photoperiod-hours × 0.0036. The day's whole light budget in one number — and your water budget tracks it closely.

The physiology is a short chain. Photosynthesis needs open stomata to let CO₂ in. Open stomata also let water vapour out — that is transpiration, and it is what pulls water up from the roots. More light means more photosynthesis, more open stomata, and more water pulled through the plant. But light only sets the demand; the rate water leaves through those open stomata is set by the vapour-pressure deficit — how thirsty the air is. Water use is the product of the two: roughly, light-driven conductance × VPD.[1][2] Turn up either and water use climbs; turn up both and it climbs fast.

VPD (vapour-pressure deficit)how much more moisture the air could hold before it saturates — the drying power of the air. High VPD pulls water out of open stomata faster; it is the multiplier on whatever demand the light created.

Across the normal operating range, transpiration tracks light and DLI close to linearly, and cannabis yield tracks light intensity the same way — right up to very high PPFD, and higher still under CO₂ enrichment.[7][8] Two things bend the line. Past a variety's light-saturation point, extra photons stop adding much photosynthesis but still add radiant heat, so water use keeps rising while growth does not — efficiency drops. And CO₂ enrichment partly closes stomata, so each leaf transpires less per unit light (better water-use efficiency) — yet the enriched room grows a bigger crop under more light and heat, so the whole-room water use usually rises, not falls. Per-leaf thrift, larger total bill.

Light also decides when water is needed, because transpiration follows the light curve through the day. This is what crop-steering phases are built around.

  1. P0 — overnight dryback. No irrigation; the plant dries the pot down in the dark. Sets how much air the roots get and how generative the morning starts.
  2. P1 — ramp / saturation. Shots after lights-on that bring the pot back up to field capacity as demand rises.
  3. P2 — maintenance. Shots through the day that hold VWC (or gently steer it) across peak transpiration.
  4. P3 — last shot. The final irrigation before lights-off, timed to set the depth of the overnight dryback.

The classic self-inflicted under-water

You raise PPFD (say 600 → 900) for more yield but leave irrigation where it was. Demand jumps ~40–50%; the pot now dries past its floor; in rockwool especially, EC spikes as it dries; the plant hits osmotic drought and wilts under the lights. It reads as heat or light stress — so the grower dims the lights instead of adding water, and throws away the yield they turned the lights up for. When you raise light, raise the water budget first — before the plant tells you.

Put it together

The daily operating loop

Everything above resolves into one loop you run every day: size the budget from the light, set the rate from the air, cap the dryback with EC, and let weight and the plant keep all three honest.

  1. 1
    Budget from DLI
    The day's light sets how much water the crop will move. Move up Table 3, move up your daily delivery. This is the coarse dial.
  2. 2
    Rate from VPD and the light curve
    Schedule shots to the shape of the day — ramp in P1, hold through the midday peak in P2, taper into P3 — with VPD deciding how fast demand comes on.
  3. 3
    Floor from the EC ceiling
    Decide how deep you will dry back — but let pore-water EC veto it. When EC hits your ceiling, that VWC is your floor, whatever the number. This is the line between steering and starving.
  4. 4
    Verify by weight and plant
    Cross-check the probe against a lift or the load-cell plant, and against turgor and canopy temperature. Disagreement is the signal to investigate — usually placement, channeling, or EC.
  5. 5
    Adjust the dryback to steer
    Deeper P0 dryback and a higher EC ceiling steer generative; shallower dryback and gentler EC steer vegetative. You are choosing where the floor sits, on purpose, not discovering it by accident.
Steering and starving are the same line, drawn with opposite intent

A controlled dryback with a known EC ceiling and a plant that recovers turgor overnight is generative steering — a floor you chose and defend. The same downward VWC line, run past the EC ceiling with a plant that wilts and does not recover, is under-watering — a floor you crossed by accident. The probe number can be identical. Always know which one you are doing.

Hard-won

Hard-won habits

The compressed version — the things that separate someone who owns sensors from someone who reads them.

  • Trend beats absolute. An uncalibrated probe still shows a perfect dryback. Calibrate for the truth of the number; use the shape from day one.
  • Weigh, don't guess. A load-cell reference plant is the single highest-value, drift-free instrument you can add. When probe and scale disagree about water, the scale wins.
  • Placement is the measurement. Middle third, root depth, one dripper-width off the emitter. The wet base and the dry edge both lie, in opposite directions.
  • Let EC set the floor. Rising pore-water EC on dryback is your real limit. When it hits your ceiling, stop drying — that VWC is the floor, whatever it reads.
  • Know how your substrate re-wets. Coco channels when it's too dry, peat repels, rockwool floods its base, clay pebbles are fine. Never let the first three go bone-dry, and recover with several small shots, never one flood.
  • Runoff EC vs feed EC is your leaching gauge. Creeping up at a steady feed = under-watering. Raise runoff % before the stack bites.
  • Morning turgor + afternoon wilt check = a free daily water audit. Limp at lights-on, or wilting that only recovers overnight, means the day's water didn't cover the day's light.
  • Keep an IR thermometer on the bench. Canopy hotter than a well-watered reference = stomata closing = stress. Confirm before you act.
  • Steer with P3, air the roots with P0. The last shot's timing sets the overnight dryback; the overnight dryback sets root oxygen and the generative/vegetative lean.
  • Watch substrate temperature at lights-on. A cold slab drinks slowly; ramp the morning shots rather than dumping cold feed into cold media.
  • Change the water before you change the lights. A DLI bump rewrites the water bill immediately; raise delivery first so the plant never has to tell you by wilting.
  • Never compare a Grodan number to a TEROS number as if they're the same scale. Different substrates, different calibrations — compare each probe only to its own history.
  • One probe is an anecdote. Run more than one per zone and treat a lone outlier as a placement problem until proven otherwise.
  • The reflex to dim or cool is usually a water problem in disguise. Check VWC, EC, and weight before you touch the environment.

The one-screen cheat sheet

Four questions answer "am I over- or under-watering?" faster than any dashboard:

How heavy?Lift it / read the scale. Light and lightening fast = under. Heavy and stuck = over.
How's the dryback?Deep, fast, floor breached = under. Flat, never recovers air = over.
Which way is EC going?Climbing = concentrating = under-watering / under-leaching. Falling = diluted / over.
What's the plant doing?Limp at lights-on, hot canopy, upward taco = under. Puffy droop, gnats, algae = over.

If you remember one sentence

Water status is weight + dryback + EC + the plant, read against the medium — each substrate holds EC and re-wets its own way — and the light bill is set by DLI × VPD, so every time you turn up the light you have already turned up the thirst.

On the numbers in this guide

Every VWC, EC, and dryback figure here is a starting band to reason from, not a setpoint to copy. Your calibration, substrate, genetics, and steering intent move all of them. Dial by watching how your own root zone responds — the method carries over; the numbers you set yourself.
